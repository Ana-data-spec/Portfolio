Time-to-Attribution: Estimating Days from Coverage Start to First PCP Assignment

A statistical analysis of how long it takes a new health plan member to be attributed to a Primary Care Provider after coverage begins — and which member segments attribute fastest or slowest.

The Problem

Health plans use claims-based logic to attribute members to a Primary Care Provider (PCP), typically based on visit patterns within a rolling window (6–24 months). The speed of that attribution matters: members who go unattributed for long stretches can fall outside care coordination programs and may be miscounted in quality measures like HEDIS. This analysis asks a simple operational question: how long does attribution actually take, and for whom does it take longest?

Approach

Built a synthetic dataset of 5,000 members with realistic coverage start dates, plan types, regions, and age groups, then modeled days-to-attribution as a right-skewed distribution adjusted by known real-world drivers: plan type (HMOs often require PCP selection at enrollment), region (rural areas have fewer PCP options), and age (older members are more likely to have an existing PCP relationship). A subset of members were modeled as never attributing within their observation window, mirroring real censoring effects.

  • Calculated the full distribution of days-to-attribution and cumulative attribution rates at 30/60/90/180-day milestones
  • Segmented results by plan type and region to identify where attribution lags
  • Built all charts in Python (pandas, numpy, matplotlib) — full script available on request

Findings

Most members who attribute do so quickly — the distribution is heavily right-skewed with a median of 34 days, but a long tail extends past 200 days.
Nearly half of members are attributed within 30 days, and the curve flattens after ~120 days — suggesting interventions should target the first month.

PPO members and rural members attribute notably slower than HMO and urban members — consistent with fewer enrollment-time PCP requirements and fewer provider options, respectively.

Takeaway

If a health plan wants to shorten time-to-attribution, the highest-leverage levers are plan design (encouraging or requiring PCP selection at enrollment, as HMOs do) and provider network access in rural areas — not just faster claims processing. This kind of segment-level view turns a single average into a set of targeted, actionable interventions.

Note on data: This project is inspired by real-world member attribution work in health plan analytics, but all data shown here is fully synthetic — generated programmatically to illustrate the method. No real member, claims, or employer data was used or referenced.

Data Quality Monitoring Dashboard for Claims & Encounters Data

A profiling and monitoring framework for catching nulls, anomalies, and inconsistencies in large healthcare datasets before they reach downstream reporting and HEDIS measures.

The Problem

Large claims and encounters datasets accumulate quality issues silently — a missing diagnosis code, an inconsistent eligibility span, a malformed provider ID — that don't surface until a downstream report breaks or a HEDIS measure comes back wrong. Catching these early, at the field level, is far cheaper than catching them after they've propagated into client-facing reporting.

Approach

Designed a profiling layer that runs against key fields in claims and encounters tables, tracking null/missing rates and a weekly composite "validation pass rate" — the share of records that pass all configured checks (required fields populated, valid code formats, eligibility spans that don't overlap, claim amounts within expected ranges).

  • Profiled 8 critical fields (Member ID, Diagnosis Code, Provider NPI, Service Date, Claim Amount, Plan Code, Eligibility Span, Place of Service) for completeness
  • Flagged fields exceeding a 3% null-rate threshold for investigation
  • Tracked a rolling weekly pass-rate trend against a 95% target, to catch degradation before it becomes a client-facing issue

Findings

Eligibility Span and Diagnosis Code carried the highest null rates — both tied to upstream source system timing issues rather than true data loss.
The composite pass rate trended upward over the 16-week window and crossed the 95% target by week 11, following upstream fixes to eligibility span logic.

Takeaway

Field-level monitoring turns "the data looks off" into a specific, trackable signal. Once Eligibility Span was identified as the main driver of failed validations, the fix was targeted at the source rather than patched downstream — which is the difference between a one-time fix and a recurring monthly firefight.

Note on data: This project is inspired by data quality and profiling work in healthcare claims analytics, but all figures, field names, and trends shown here are illustrative and synthetic. No real client data, table structures, or volumes are represented.

Retail Return Fraud Busters: Clustering Suspicious Return Behavior

A K-means clustering model to detect fraudulent return behavior from transaction-level retail data. Awarded 1st place for analytical innovation.

The Problem

Retail return fraud — excessive returns, "wardrobing," and return-for-refund schemes — costs retailers billions annually, but most fraud isn't a single suspicious transaction; it's a pattern across many transactions from the same customer. The challenge: separate genuinely high-return customers from those exhibiting a distinct, suspicious behavioral signature.

Approach

Used unsupervised learning rather than fraud labels (which weren't available) to let the data reveal natural groupings of customer behavior.

  • Engineered features per customer: return frequency over a 90-day window and average return value
  • Applied K-means clustering to segment customers into behavioral groups
  • Identified a distinct cluster of high-frequency, high-value returners as the highest-risk segment for manual review

Findings

A clear behavioral cluster emerged with both high return frequency and high average return value — a combination far rarer among normal shoppers.
Of 860 customers analyzed, 60 (~7%) fell into the flagged segment — a manageable shortlist for manual fraud review rather than a blanket policy change.

Takeaway

Clustering surfaced a actionable, narrow list for investigation instead of a noisy rule-based flag system. This approach won 1st place in the competition for combining a simple, interpretable model with a result a non-technical fraud team could immediately act on.

Note on data: Original hackathon dataset is not available for redistribution; this writeup uses a synthetic dataset built to reproduce the same clustering result and findings from the competition.

U.S. University Diversity Visualization

Interactive dashboards built in Tableau and Power BI to make enrollment diversity trends legible to non-technical university stakeholders.

The Problem

Enrollment diversity data is often published as dense tables that university administrators and policy stakeholders don't have time to parse. The goal was to translate multi-year, multi-institution enrollment data into visuals a non-technical audience could read in seconds, not minutes.

Approach

Pulled and cleaned multi-year enrollment data by race/ethnicity across U.S. institutions, then built two complementary views: a year-over-year shift comparison and a regional diversity index using a Simpson Diversity Index calculation (a standard measure of how evenly distributed a population is across categories).

  • Built interactive dashboards in both Tableau and Power BI to compare tool tradeoffs for this audience
  • Calculated a Simpson Diversity Index per region to quantify "how diverse," not just "what % of each group"
  • Designed visuals specifically for non-technical readers — direct labels, minimal jargon, clear before/after framing

Findings

Hispanic and Asian student enrollment share grew the most between 2015 and 2023, while the White student share declined by roughly 9 percentage points.
The West region showed the highest diversity index, while the Midwest showed the lowest — useful context for institutions benchmarking against regional peers rather than national averages alone.

Takeaway

A single index number plus a clear before/after chart communicated more to stakeholders in one meeting than the full underlying dataset would have in a week of follow-up emails — a reminder that the right visualization is itself a form of analysis.
